How to Digitize Old Letters and Photos Without Ruining Them

The box has waited thirty years. It can wait one more weekend — but not another thirty.

Last updated July 12, 2026

Every family has the box. Photographs with scalloped edges, letters in fading ballpoint, a wedding telegram, somebody's report card from 1962. Everyone agrees it's irreplaceable, and it's protected by absolutely nothing except a lid and good luck. One basement flood, one house fire, one confused spring cleaning — and it's gone, entirely. Digitizing it is one of those projects that's genuinely easier than it looks; the trick is knowing the handful of things that actually matter and ignoring the rest.

Before anything touches a scanner

Old paper forgives less than it used to. A few handling habits protect it while you work:

  • Clean, dry hands beat gloves for paper. Cotton gloves reduce your grip and cause more fumbles than they prevent smudges. The exception is photographs and negatives — hold those by the edges, since fingerprints on emulsion are close to permanent.
  • Work on a cleared table, not the sofa. Coffee and a seventy-year-old letter should never share a surface. It sounds obvious right up until it happens.
  • Don't undo anything by force. Rusted paperclips, brittle rubber bands, photos stuck to each other or to album pages — slow down there. Slide paperclips off sideways. If two photos have fused, don't peel; scan the pair as-is and consider it a two-for-one.
  • Unfold letters gently and once. Paper that's been folded for fifty years wants to break at the crease. Open it enough to scan, and skip the urge to press it flat under a stack of books while it's brittle.
  • Those "magnetic" albums from the 70s and 80s are the enemy. The adhesive yellows photos and grips them harder every year. Unwaxed dental floss worked behind a corner will free most prints; the truly stuck ones should be scanned right through the album page. Hazy beats torn.

Phone or scanner? An honest answer

You'll read purist advice claiming phone scanning is sacrilege. Ignore it. The right tool depends on what the scan is for.

A phone with a scanning app, used in good indirect light, produces images that are completely fine for sharing in the family group chat, attaching to a reconnection message, or making a casual photo book. It costs nothing, and its speed means the whole box actually gets done — a finished phone pass beats a flatbed project that stalls at 4%.

A flatbed scanner earns its place when the goal is preservation: no glare, no keystone distortion, real resolution, and consistent color. It's also the only reasonable route for slides and negatives, which phones handle badly. If the box holds mixed formats — prints plus a sleeve of negatives plus grandpa's slide carousels — look for a flatbed built to scan film as well as paper (the Epson Perfection V600 is the standard recommendation for exactly this, and one of the few consumer machines that does both well). It's not fast — slides in particular are a podcast-and-patience job — but it's the difference between "we have a picture of the picture" and "we have the picture."

The sensible hybrid, and what most people should actually do: phone-scan everything in one or two sittings so a copy exists at all, then flatbed the fifty to hundred items that matter most.

Settings, minus the rabbit hole

Scanner software offers forty options; three decisions cover it. Scan photos at 600 dpi — enough to reprint larger than the original and capture what the print actually holds. Letters and documents are fine at 300 dpi, which keeps handwriting crisp without bloating files. Slides and negatives need 2400 dpi or higher, because you're enlarging something the size of a stamp. Save as JPEG at high quality; archivists prefer TIFF, and if a particular image is the family treasure, by all means keep a TIFF of it, but a high-quality JPEG of everything beats perfect TIFFs of the twelve items you scanned before abandoning the project. And scan the backs of photos whenever anything is written there — "Helen & Ruth, Lake George, Aug '54" in someone's handwriting is half the artifact.

Letters want a little extra care

Photographs explain themselves; letters need their context kept together. Scan the envelope with its letter — the postmark is often the only date, and the address in a familiar hand is part of the object. Name multi-page letters so they stay in order and together ("1943-06-12_frank-to-mary_p1" and so on). And for the letters that matter most, consider typing out a transcription into a plain text file alongside the scans; faded pencil and antique cursive get harder to read every generation, and a transcript is the difference between descendants who can read the letter and descendants who do.

If what you're holding is a deceased parent's or grandparent's correspondence and the sorting itself feels heavy, our guide on a loved one's letters and photos deals with that side of it — the emotional logistics this article is deliberately skipping.

Naming files so future-you can find anything

The failure mode here isn't bad scans; it's a folder of four hundred files named IMG_20260712_0038.jpg. You don't need software or a database. You need a folder per era or family branch ("1950s-mom-childhood," "korea-letters," "1978-1985-albums") and a date-first filename with two or three useful words: "1954-08_lake-george_helen-ruth.jpg." Approximate dates are fine — "1950s" sorts well enough. Then add the one file nobody thinks of: a plain text file in the top folder called WHO-IS-WHO.txt, explaining the nicknames, the maiden names, and which of the three Roberts is which. Twenty minutes of typing, and it will someday be the most valuable file in the archive.

While you're identifying faces: if older relatives are alive who can name the strangers in these photos, that window is closing. Sit with them and the scans for an afternoon. You'll leave with names for the mystery bridesmaids and, usually, six stories nobody had ever told you.

Back it up like you mean it

A scan that exists in one place is a rumor. The photographer's rule of thumb — three copies, two kinds of storage, one somewhere else — sounds elaborate and takes an hour: the working copy on your computer, a copy on an external drive (a WD My Passport is the boring, correct choice), and a copy in any cloud service you already use. The cloud copy is the quiet hero: it's the one that survives the same house fire the box wouldn't have. Sharing a copy of the whole archive with a sibling in another city accomplishes the same thing and doubles as a very good reason to call your sibling.

The originals, afterward

Scanning doesn't retire the originals — handwriting, paper, the object your grandmother actually held — but it does change the job from "preserve everything" to "keep the meaningful things well." The keepers deserve better than the shoebox: an album built without the acid and PVC that slowly yellow paper (the Pioneer acid-free archival albums are inexpensive and made for this) or a proper keepsake box somewhere cool, dry, and not the attic or the basement — both of which are temperature-swing and moisture disasters for paper.

And the duplicates, the blurry thirds of every shot, the receipts from 1981? Once good scans exist in two places, you're allowed to let physical items go. Some families instead mail them onward — the duplicate prints of cousins to those cousins, with a two-line note. It empties the box and, more than occasionally, restarts a correspondence.

One last nudge: pick the best hundred scans and have them printed as a book (a Mixbook photo book handles this well), because a folder is an archive but a book gets handed around a Thanksgiving table. The scanning protects the memories. The book is what makes anyone look at them.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a phone camera good enough to digitize old photos?

For sharing with family, yes — a phone scanning app in good light produces perfectly usable images. For preservation, a flatbed scanner is meaningfully better: higher resolution, no glare or perspective distortion, and the ability to handle slides and negatives. Many people sensibly do both: phone for the quick pass, scanner for the hundred that matter most.

What resolution should I scan old photos and letters at?

600 dpi for photographs (it lets you reprint at larger sizes later and captures nearly everything the print holds) and 300 dpi for letters and documents, which keeps handwriting fully legible at reasonable file sizes. Slides and negatives need much higher settings, 2400 dpi or more, because the originals are so small.

How do I remove photos stuck in old magnetic albums?

Gently, and sometimes not at all. Try sliding unwaxed dental floss behind a corner with slow pressure. If a photo won't release, scan or photograph it in place through the album page rather than forcing it — a slightly hazy image beats a torn original. Never pull a stuck photo straight up.

Should I keep the original letters and photos after scanning them?

Keep the meaningful ones — originals carry handwriting, paper, and presence no scan reproduces, and they belong in acid-free storage. But you don't have to keep everything. Once a good scan exists in two places, letting go of duplicates and marginal items is reasonable, and the scan preserves the story.
