What to Say at a Reunion When It's Been Twenty Years

For the five minutes in the parking lot when you consider driving home.

Last updated July 12, 2026

Photo by roanokecollege, licensed under CC BY 2.0

Everyone imagines reunions as one long warm hug, and parts of them are. But there's also the moment a vaguely familiar face lights up at you and starts talking, and you have no idea who this person is. And the moment someone asks what you've been up to, and twenty years compresses into panicked silence. This is a guide to those moments — the actual sentences.

One reassurance before the scripts: every single person in that room rehearsed something in the car. The confident-looking ones too. Especially them.

The question you already know is coming

"So what have you been up to?" will be asked of you between five and forty times, and winging it goes badly in two directions — the fifteen-minute unabridged version, or the deer-in-headlights "oh, you know... stuff." Prepare a two-sentence answer. One sentence of facts, one sentence with a human being in it, and a question handed back:

"I'm in Phoenix now — two kids, and I do IT for a hospital chain. My actual life is coaching my daughter's softball team, which I'm statistically the worst coach in the league at. What about you — did you end up leaving for the coast like you always said?"

That last clause is the good stuff. Remembering something they said or wanted at eighteen — even approximately — is the single most disarming move available at a reunion. It says: you weren't background to me.

And if your twenty years contain hard chapters — divorce, a lost job, a lost person — you get to decide the depth in real time. A light-but-honest version ("It's been a real decade, I'll tell you the whole saga if you buy me a coffee sometime") acknowledges without unloading, and more than one real coffee has come from exactly that sentence.

When you have no idea who this is

It will happen. Faces change more in twenty-five years than anyone's mental yearbook accounts for. The instinct is to stall and fish for clues; the stall is always more insulting than the truth. Go fast and warm instead:

"Okay, I need you to rescue me — I know exactly who you are and my brain refuses to produce the name. It's been doing this all night."

Nobody has ever been genuinely wounded by that sentence at a reunion, because they've spent the evening two syllables from needing it themselves. If the organizers were kind enough to make name tags with yearbook photos, thank them. If you're the one who blanked and got rescued, return the favor for someone else within the hour; it's the reunion economy.

The heavier conversations, briefly

A reunion crowd carries everything a couple decades deposits on people, and some conversations need more than banter.

The classmate who lost someone. If you know their spouse or parent or child died, don't dodge it — the dodge is louder than the mention. One sentence, then follow their lead: "I heard about Mark. I'm so sorry. I still remember him at your wedding doing that ridiculous toast." If they want to talk, they will; if they pivot to the buffet line, go with them graciously. The mention itself was the gift.

The person you fell out with. A party is not the venue for relitigating 2007. If you find yourselves face to face, short and warm does it: "Hey. It's genuinely good to see you." Full stop. If you want more than a truce, that's a conversation for afterward — our guide on rebuilding a friendship after a falling-out covers doing it properly.

The person you weren't kind to. Some people arrive at reunions carrying guilt about who they were at sixteen. If that's you, a brief, no-strings acknowledgment can mean more than you'd guess: "I've thought more than once about how I treated you back then, and I'm sorry. You didn't deserve it." Then let them respond however they respond — including coolly. You're not owed absolution; you're settling your own account.

The old flame. Warmth is fine; nostalgia after three drinks is a lane change. "It's really good to see you" carries everything worth carrying. If either of you is married, that's also where it ends, and the classy version of you knows it before the second drink instead of after.

Escaping a conversation without wounding anyone

You'll get pinned down — by the class talker, by someone selling something, by a perfectly nice person during the exact window your actual best friend from those years is putting on her coat. Exits that leave no mark:

  • "I promised myself I'd say hi to Renata before she leaves — but I'm so glad we got to catch up."
  • "I'm going to make a round before the night gets away from me. Find me on the way out?"
  • The classic, still undefeated: "I'm getting a refill — can I grab you anything?" (You are not obligated to return with a beverage and a continuation.)

Naming a destination is the trick. A person with somewhere to be is leaving a conversation; a person without one is escaping it, and everyone can tell the difference.

The follow-up is the whole point

Here's the quiet failure built into most reunions: a dozen genuine "we should get together!" moments, zero calendar entries. The reunion doesn't reconnect you — it produces the excuse to reconnect, with a 72-hour expiration on its warmth.

So, sometime before Wednesday, send the two or three messages that matter. Reference the conversation, not the event, and make one concrete offer:

"Still laughing about the story of the tent. I meant it about coffee — I'm downtown Tuesdays and Thursdays, pick one and I'm buying."

That's it. The people who leave reunions with rekindled friendships aren't the ones who had the best conversations Saturday night; they're the ones who sent the boring little logistics text on Monday. From there, our guides on reconnecting with an old friend and staying in touch once you have take over.

If you're thinking about not going

The most common private reason people skip reunions isn't scheduling. It's the feeling of being behind — not thin enough, not far enough along, not who eighteen-year-old-you promised. Two things about that. First: the people whose lives look gleaming on Instagram feel a version of the same dread, because everyone's yardstick was calibrated by the same seventeen-year-olds. Second: nobody at a decent reunion is conducting an audit. They're scanning the room for faces that were kind to them a long time ago. Yours is one of those faces for someone, exactly as it is.

And if the dread still wins — that's allowed. A reunion is one format for reconnection, not a moral obligation. Skipping the ballroom and writing directly to the two people you actually miss is not a lesser choice. Some years it's the better one.

Frequently Asked Questions

What do I say when I don't recognize someone at a reunion?

Admit it fast and warmly — “I'm so sorry, twenty years has completely rewired my memory for faces. Tell me your name and I promise the rest will come back.” Everyone in the room has the same fear, so honesty lands as relief, not insult. Stalling and hoping for a clue almost always goes worse.

How should I answer “so what have you been up to?” at a reunion?

Prepare a two-sentence answer before you go: one sentence of facts, one sentence with something human or funny in it, ending in a question back. The goal isn't to impress; it's to keep the conversation moving past the résumé exchange to something actually enjoyable.

Should I go to my reunion if I feel like I haven't accomplished enough?

That worry is close to universal — even the people who look successful online feel a version of it. Nobody at a good reunion is auditing careers; they're looking for familiar faces. If the dread outweighs the curiosity, though, skipping is allowed, and reaching out individually to the two or three people you actually miss is a legitimate substitute.

How do I follow up with someone I reconnected with at a reunion?

Within a few days, while the warmth is fresh — reference the specific conversation you had, not just the event, and suggest one concrete low-key next step, like coffee or a phone call. A quick “great to see you” text sent Monday beats a perfect message sent in October.
