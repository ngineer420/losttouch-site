How to Reconnect With a Former Mentor or Teacher

They probably never found out how your story went. You're holding the ending.

Last updated July 12, 2026

Photo by Markus Reinhardt, licensed under CC BY 2.0

There's a particular person a lot of us carry around: the teacher who took you seriously before anyone else did, the coach who benched you at exactly the right moment, the first boss who let you fail safely. You quote them without noticing. And somewhere in the back of your mind is a debt you've never paid, because saying thank you twenty years late feels strange, and every year it feels a little stranger.

Here's the thing that changes the math: teachers and mentors almost never get endings.

A teacher meets you at fifteen and loses track of you at eighteen. A mentor helps you through your first real job and then you change cities. From their side, nearly every story they've ever invested in just... stops. They spend whole careers planting things they'll never see come up. Which means the message you're nervous about sending isn't an interruption of their day. It's the rarest thing in their professional life — a report from the future, delivered by the one person qualified to write it.

What to actually say

Three ingredients, and specificity does most of the work in all of them:

  1. One specific moment. Not "you were a great teacher" — a scene. The day they kept you after class, the note in the margin, the sentence they said that you can still hear. They were there for thousands of moments; show them the one that stuck.
  2. What it turned into. This is the part only you can supply: the line from that moment to your life now. The career, the habit, the way you talk to your own kids or your own students. It doesn't have to be dramatic. "I still outline essays the way you taught us" is a completely satisfying ending to a story they never got to finish.
  3. The plain thank you. Unhedged, no irony. One sentence. You'd be surprised how many drafts bury it.

Here's the whole thing assembled, a letter to a high school teacher after twenty-two years:

Dear Ms. Okafor,

You probably won't remember me — Jordan Reyes, your fourth-period English class, 2003 to 2004, the kid who sat by the window and never talked.

In November of that year you handed back an essay of mine with one line written across the top: "You're a writer. Act like it." I want you to know I've thought about that sentence at least once a month for twenty years. I didn't become a novelist or anything cinematic — I write grants for a hospital system — but I write for a living, and I trace it directly to that margin note. Nobody had ever named it before you did.

I'm writing partly because I recently caught myself saying "you're a writer, act like it" to my own daughter about her sketchbooks, and I realized I'd never once said thank you to the person I stole it from.

So: thank you. It mattered more than you could have known from where you stood.

With real gratitude,
Jordan Reyes

Notice the letter doesn't need her to remember Jordan. It gives her the name, year, and seat, then moves on — the letter works whether she remembers the window kid or not. Build yours the same way. Memory is the teacher's problem to enjoy, not a test your letter has to pass.

If it was a work mentor, not a teacher

Same skeleton, different pitfall. When you write to a former boss or professional mentor, there's a decent chance they'll brace for the networking ask — that's what most out-of-the-blue messages in their inbox turn out to be. So close that door explicitly, and early:

"Hi Raj — no agenda here, and I promise I'm not about to ask for a reference. I was training a new analyst this week and heard myself repeating, word for word, the thing you told me in 2014 about assuming the data is wrong until it proves otherwise. It's the single most useful sentence anyone's said to me professionally, and I realized I never told you that. I hope things are good at Meridian — and genuinely, thank you for the patience you spent on me back then. It compounded."

If you do also want career advice someday, fine — but not in this message. Send the thanks, let it stand alone for a few months, and let any future ask be its own conversation. Gratitude with an invoice attached isn't gratitude, and experienced mentors can smell the difference instantly.

Finding them, decades later

Teachers can be surprisingly findable, even long-retired ones:

  • The school itself. Front offices routinely forward mail to retired staff, and many keep loose track of where beloved teachers ended up. A short call — "I'm a former student trying to get a thank-you letter to Mr. Halvorsen, could you forward it?" — is a request school secretaries genuinely enjoy.
  • Class and hometown Facebook groups. "Does anyone know how to reach Coach D?" posted in a "You grew up in Mishawaka if..." group has an astonishing hit rate. Half the time the coach is in the group.
  • Classmates who stayed local. Somebody from your year still lives in town and ran into your teacher at the pharmacy last spring. Find that classmate.
  • For professors: emeritus faculty usually keep a university email for life, listed on a department page nobody has updated since 2011. It still works.
  • For work mentors: LinkedIn, obviously — and a message there is fine, though moving to email quickly makes it feel less like professional maintenance.

If those run dry, the broader toolkit in our guide to finding someone's contact info respectfully applies here too. And paper deserves special consideration for this particular relationship: a teacher who's been retired for fifteen years may check email rarely, but a handwritten letter forwarded by the school will absolutely reach the kitchen table. What happens next, honestly

Often: a warm, slightly overwhelmed reply, sometimes weeks later, sometimes with your old essay's grade recalled with suspicious precision. Occasionally a correspondence starts — a coffee when you're back home, a standing email exchange, a second act for the relationship as something closer to friendship between adults. Those are lovely, and they're real; plenty of people have a former teacher at their wedding.

Sometimes, though, you'll get a kind but brief note, or nothing. An elderly teacher may not manage correspondence anymore. A busy mentor may read it, feel genuinely moved, and never find the moment to answer. Send the letter in a way that makes peace with this up front — it's a delivery of something owed, not an application for something wanted. The letter completes itself the moment it's read.

And one harder possibility deserves saying plainly, because the longer you wait the more likely it becomes: they may have died. If you find an obituary instead of an address, the letter is still worth writing — sent to their spouse or children. "Your mother changed the direction of my life, and here's how" is a letter families keep forever. It's not the ending you wanted, but it isn't nothing. It might even be more than you know.

A caveat worth sitting with

Not every influential figure was a good one, and time has a way of gilding people who don't deserve it. If what you remember alongside the inspiration is fear, humiliation, or boundaries that look worse the older you get, you don't owe that person a thank-you letter — nostalgia is not a debt. Some people find it more honest to thank the good teachers and let the complicated ones stay unwritten-to. That's not a failure of gratitude. It's accuracy.

For everyone else — the teacher whose voice you still hear, kindly — the letter takes about half an hour, costs a stamp, and will likely be read more than once. Somewhere out there is a retired teacher with a drawer where letters like yours are kept. Very few things you write this year will outlast that drawer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it weird to contact a teacher after ten or twenty years?

No — for most teachers it's one of the best messages they receive all year. Teachers rarely learn how their students' stories turned out, and a letter that tells them is a genuine gift, not an imposition. Retired teachers in particular tend to treasure them.

What should I say to a former mentor or teacher?

Three things: one specific moment or piece of advice you still remember, what it turned into in your life, and a plain thank you. Specificity matters most — “you taught me to write a topic sentence and I still hear your voice when I edit” lands far harder than a general “you were a great teacher.”

What if my teacher doesn't remember me?

Write the message so it doesn't depend on being remembered. Give them your name, the year, and the class up front, then let the letter be about what they did rather than testing their memory. A teacher who taught thousands of students can still be genuinely moved by proof that one of them carried something forward.

How do I find a retired teacher or old mentor?

Start with the school itself — front offices and alumni associations often forward letters to retired staff. Class Facebook groups, other students from that era, LinkedIn for professional mentors, and a plain search of their name plus the school usually cover the rest.
